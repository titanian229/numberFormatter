Posting to the Ledger

You’ve learned that there are advantages to recording transactions in a journal. You know that the journal functions like a blank book or spreadsheet where you record all transactions.

For each transaction, the journal shows the date, account description, whether the entry is a debit or a credit, and an explanation. However, the journal does not indicate the balance for each account. It does not show you how much cash you have or how much a particular customer owes the business. This type of useful information is required by all businesses and is found in the individual accounts. That is why, after the transactions have been recorded in the journal, they are transferred to individual ledger accounts.

Transferring business transaction information from the journal to the individual accounts is called posting to the ledger accounts.

In this learning activity, you will learn how to post transactions to their respective ledger accounts. You will also learn how to open accounts for a new business. Finally, you will put this all together by performing a trial balance and financial statements.

Having access to each account is important for managing a business. But how do we keep these accounts organized to ensure they are easy to access and understand?

How to post the November 1 cash sale transaction

These are the steps followed in posting the first part of this entry to the Cash account in the ledger.

Step 1: Locate the Cash account.

It is account 100 in the ledger.

Step 2: Record the date of the transaction in the Cash account.

The month and year need only be recorded once on each page.

Step 3: Enter the debit amount.

The amount of $700 is written in the debit column of the account.

Step 4: Calculate the new cash balance.

Since the previous balance was $1000 debit (noted on November 1) and another debit of $700 is being placed in the account, the new balance is $1700 (debit). The $1700 is written in the balance column and DR is written in the DR/CR column to indicate that the balance is a debit balance.

Step 5: Complete the ledger’s posting reference column in the account.

The reference is the journal page from which the entry was posted, so J48 is written in the posting reference (P.R.) column of the ledger, to indicate that the entry came from page 48 of the journal.

Step 6: Complete the journal’s posting reference column.

The reference is the ledger account number to which the entry was transferred. Therefore 100 is written in the posting reference (P.R.) column of the journal.

Posting References

Posting reference numbers cross-reference the link from the journal entries to the corresponding postings to ledger accounts.

  • The journal page number documented in the P.R. column in ledger account tells you where the journal entry can be found that has more information about the transaction (for example, the name of the source document).
  • The ledger account number documented in the general journal in the P.R. column expresses that the amount has been posted in the ledger account. By doing this, the accounting clerk knows where he/she left off.
  • The cross-reference helps an auditor check the accuracy of the journalizing and posting of transactions. Posting references allow a trace from the journal to the ledger, and from the ledger to the journal.

Opening the Books

You’ve learned the advantages of the balance-column form of ledger account and how to post transactions from the journal to the accounts. Now you’ll examine the procedure for getting an accounting system started, which is called opening the books. Remember our friend Clara Chow? She would use a system like this one when getting her business started.

The Opening Entry

The first entry—the opening entry—is made when a business starts operations.

Leslie Roberts is opening up her own law firm. On February 1, Leslie Roberts borrows $15 000 from a bank. With the bank loan and her own money, she purchases assets and starts her law firm.

The assets of the new business are:

Cash $10 000

Office Supplies $2 000

Equipment $30 000

Total Assets equal (10 000 + 2 000 + 30 000) = $42 000

The business has one liability:

Bank Loan $15 000

Owner’s equity is calculated using the balance sheet equation: A = L + OE

OE = A - L

$27 000 = $42 000 - $15 000

Roberts’ equity is $27 000.

To open the books, the new business’s assets, liabilities, and owner’s equity are recorded in the general journal. This first entry is called the opening entry.

General Journal Page 1
Date
20--		 Accounts and Explanation P.R. Debit Credit
Feb. 1 Cash 10 000
Office Supplies 2 000
Equipment 30 000
Bank Loan 15 000
L. Roberts, Capital 27 000
Started a new accounting firm with the above assets, liability and owner's equity.

In this opening entry, note that:

  • the total of the debits equals the total of the credits
  • when there are multiple debits and credits, the entry is called a compound journal entry

Opening Ledger Accounts

After the opening entry is recorded in the general journal, the ledger accounts are opened and the opening entry is posted.

The steps in this process are:

  1. Write the name of the account. A separate page is required for each account.
  2. Write the account number. This is determined from the chart of accounts.
  3. Write the date in the date column.
  4. Write “Opening Entry” on the first line of the “Item/ Particulars” column.
  5. Write J1 (journal, page 1) in the posting reference column (PR).
  6. Record the amount in the correct debit or credit column.
  7. Enter the balance in the balance column.
  8. Examine the balance and write DR or CR in the DR/CR column.
  9. Enter the ledger account number in the posting reference column of the general journal.
  10. Insert the accounts in numerical sequence in the ledger.

Forwarding Procedure

After several transactions, you will fill up an account page in the general ledger. When this happens:

  • Start a new page and head it up with the same account name and number.
  • Write “Forwarded” on the last line of the old page in the “Item/Particulars” column.
  • Write the date in the date column.
  • Write “Forwarded” in the “Item/Particulars” column of the new page.
  • Place the balance from the old page in the balance column.
  • Place a check mark in the posting reference column.

Reviewing the Trial Balance

As you already know, a trial balance is proof of the mathematical accuracy of the ledger. A trial balance is prepared regularly, usually once a month, just before the financial statements are prepared. It lists the debit and credit account balances in the order in which they appear in the ledger.

The balance-column form of ledger accounts makes it easy to prepare a trial balance. Each account has an up-to-date balance and the DR/CR column tells you in which column of the trial balance the balance is recorded.

The total of the debit balances should equal the total of the credit balances.

The Financial Statements

After the trial balance, the financial statements are prepared.

Which of the statements do you think is prepared first: the income statement or the balance sheet?

The income statement must be done first, in order to determine whether there has been a profit or a loss. The amount of the profit or loss is then used to adjust the Owner’s Capital account on the balance sheet. You will examine this in more depth in the next section of this lesson.

Equity Accounts on the Balance Sheet

You now know that there are two owner’s equity accounts. The Owner’s Capital account indicates the owner’s investment in the business. The Owner’s Drawings account records assets withdrawn from the business (including cash) for the owner’s personal use. The Capital account increases when a profit (net income) is made and it decreases when a loss (net loss) occurs. At the end of each accounting period, the net income determined on the income statement is added to the capital on the balance sheet.

If the owner has withdrawn assets during the accounting period, there will be a debit balance in the Drawings account. This decreases the owner’s investment in the business and, like a net loss, is subtracted from the capital on the balance sheet.

Let’s take a look at what the owner’s equity section of a balance sheet would look like with profit (net income) and withdrawals by the owner. In October, the business had a net income of $12 000 and the owner, J. Korvo withdrew $5 000 for personal use.

Less: J. Korvo, Drawings 5 000

J. Korvo, Capital, October 31 $85 000

In November, the business had a smaller net income of $2000. J. Korvo withdrew the $5000 for personal use in November. It could eventually become less than zero, at which time assets must be less than liabilities (remember the accounting equation). This would indicate that the company is in trouble and could go out of business.

The Accounting Cycle

You know that the accounting period is the time period covered by the financial statements. Most businesses prepare monthly financial statements. They are prepared this often (or more often) so that the owners and managers know how well the business is doing, and whether changes need to be made in the operation of the business.

The accounting cycle lists the steps or functions that are completed during the accounting period. These steps must be completed if the financial statements are to be correct.

Steps in the Accounting Cycle

Here are the steps in the accounting cycle:

Step 1: As business transactions occur, source documents are prepared.

Step 2: Source documents are recorded in the journal.

Step 3: The journal transactions are posted to the ledger accounts.

Step 4: A trial balance is prepared to prove the accuracy of the ledger.

Step 5: The financial statements are prepared.

You will learn additional steps as you proceed through the course. Great work so far!
